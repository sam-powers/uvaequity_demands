ANALYSIS

We collected 50 years of documents written by Students, Faculty, Staff, and Working Groups, all addressing racial inequity at the University of Virginia. Our team of qualitative researchers read each document, broke them down into specific demands, and grouped those demands into categories to understand concrete domains for institutional change.

These categories range from specific actions like Minority Student Recruitment to Living Wages for Staff Members to more genaral comments around categories such as School Climate .

As we prepared this project, we had to consistently remind ourselves that repeated asks, longer documents, and louder voices are not always indicative of the most urgent or important actions. One pithy demand does not take up as much space as a ten point plan. But it can bear as much power. Not to mention people are often pragmatic. Sometimes demands represent what people feel will be accomplishable without giving voice to their deepest hopes and dreams.

As you engage with our work, think of this as a conversation. It is not an exclusive or exhaustive list of the work to be done nor a comprehensive poll of public opinion. Instead, it is a record of history to honor those who envisioned what the University could be long before it accepted that vision for itself.

The Document raises 4 key themes:
School Climate Minority Student Recruitment Living Wages Abolition/ Defunding
We can see that Minority Student Recruitment was central to its mission in 1970 and was subsequently expanded in 1985 even as other themes joined the conversation
In contrast, themes of Abolition/ Defunding dissappeared in the 1987 conversations and were not raised again prominently until 2015.
In 1987, the first Audacious Faith Report introduced several new themes while building off those from before. Reading the documents, we can see how the top theme, School Climate, pulls on unmet demands from the Referendum and then adds more discrete action items.

Above, we looked at when specific topics entered conversation. Next we will look at who said them. Which groups speak on which issues? Who speaks together?

One of our more striking findins is the degree to which the University speaks about itself in the form of working groups and Presedential commissions. Excuding points documented in the 2017 Index of Diversity Recommendations (a University document summarizing prior student documents), we recorded 330 discrete actions demanded of the University by itself. A Few of Our Takeaways

Thought Leaders: Did you see that Black Students at the University have spoken as an identity group on every key topic here but one? Next to the University, they are the most prolific demographic in this set. In particular, Black Students have led the converstation on UVA's relationship with the Charlottesville Community. Take a look at Community Labor or Partnership Current Initiatives & Working Groups & Living Wages.

Money Matters: The University speaks alot in this set, but not on Abolition/ Defunding a category which includes Divestment/Responsible Corporate Finance & Removal of Statues. That said, faculty groups have broached the topic before.

Precision, Not Breadth: There is alot of literature here. As we noted at the start, sometimes forcing comparisons based on written quantity of discrete demands deeemphasizes the important contribution of a pithy document. For example, visually looking at the graphic above, you might not have spent alot of time looking at APIDA and Latinx contributions. And yet, the #We Are Not Invisible and Our University To Shape documents have been integral to starting conversations among the other groups. Use the Visual Library to look for these types of sparks.

Conversational Consensus: All groups have spoken on the need for Minority Faculty / Staff Recruitment and Retention and some restructuring of Research or Coursework toward a more antiracist and inclusive bent.
