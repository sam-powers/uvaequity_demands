ANALYSIS

We collected 50 years of documents written by Students, Faculty, Staff, and Working Groups, all addressing racial inequity at the University of Virginia. Our team of qualitative researchers read each document, broke them down into specific demands, and grouped those demands into categories to understand concrete domains for institutional change.

These categories range from specific actions like Minority Student Recruitment to Living Wage for Staff Members to more genaral comments around categories such as School Climate . For a closer look at the categories and their meanings, take a look at our Methodology page.

As we prepared this project, we had to consistently remind ourselves that repeated asks, longer documents, and louder voices are not always indicative of the most urgent or important actions. One pithy demand does not take up as much space as a ten point plan. But it can bear as much power. Not to mention people are often pragmatic. Sometimes demands represent what people feel will be accomplishable without giving voice to their deepest hopes and dreams.

As you engage with our work, think of this as a conversation. It is not an exclusive or exhaustive list of the work to be done nor a comprehensive poll of public opinion. Instead, it is a record of history to honor those who envisioned what the University could be long before it started to accept that vision for itself.

Who Speaks?

Above, we looked at when specific topics enter the conversation. But we also need to consider who said them. Which groups speak on which issues? Who speaks together? So we grouped the documents by six author categories: (1) Faculty/Staff, (2) University Offices & Commissions, (3) Black Students at UVA, (4) APIDA Students at UVA, (5) Latinx Students at UVA, & (6) Groups of students who do not specificy demographic affiliation. We made these classifications using the authors' self-identification in the documents themselves. We then summed the number of recommendations made in each category by each group over time.

Tabulating these recommendations by authors, we were struck by the number of times the University spoke about itself in the form of working groups and Presedential commissions. Excluding recommendations recorded in the 2017 Index of Diversity Recommendations (a University document summarizing prior student documents), we recorded 330 discrete actions demanded of the University by itself.   • For groups that do not have many written records, what have they spoken about?
  • In what ways does our focus here on number of actions demanded deemphasize other important factors?

A Few of Our Takeaways

Thought (& Action) Leaders: Did you see that Black students at the University have spoken as an identity group on every key topic here but one (Reparations)? Next to the University, they are the most prolific demographic in this set. In particular, Black students have led the converstation on UVA's relationship with the Charlottesville Community. Take a look at Community Labor or Partnership Current Initiatives and Working Groups & Living Wage.

Money Matters: The University speaks alot in this set, but not on Abolition/ Defunding, a category that includes Divestment/Responsible Corporate Finance & Removal of Statues. That said, faculty groups have broached the topic before.

Precision, Not Breadth: There is alot of literature here. As we noted at the start, sometimes forcing comparisons based on written quantity of discrete demands deeemphasizes the important contribution of pithy documents or activism outside of the written word. For example, visually looking at the graphic above, you might not have spent alot of time examining APIDA and Latinx contributions. And yet, the #We Are Not Invisible and Our University To Shape documents sparked so much conversation among other groups represented in this analysis. They made precise and actionable asks. Use the Visual Library to look for other instances of poignant but powerful speech.

Conversational Consensus: All groups have spoken on the need for Minority Faculty / Staff Recruitment and Retention and some restructuring of Research or Coursework toward a more antiracist and inclusive bent.


What Are They Saying?

After knowing who spoke on which topics, we wanted to get a sense for what types of words were used in each category. Specifically, we wanted to know what key terms and actions were distinctive or most unique to each category. To do that, we used a metric called keyness.

Keyness compares the distribution of words in one set to the distribution of words in another. You can think of it as a comparative measure of occurence. For example, if we were comparing fruit in Florida to fruit in other states, Pineapples would probably have a high keyness in Florida. If you are interested, you can read more about keyness here.

We calculated keyness and chose the top ten most distinctive words in each category. We display those distinctive words in the circular bar chart below. Bars are ordered from highest to lowest keyness. You can hover over each bar to see the key word as well as the number of times that word was used in the demand category.
